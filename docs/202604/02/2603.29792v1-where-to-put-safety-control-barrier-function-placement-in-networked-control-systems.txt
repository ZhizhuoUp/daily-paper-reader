# Where to Put Safety? Control Barrier Function Placement in Networked Control Systems 

Severin Beger, Yuling Chen, and Sandra Hirche 

Abstract — Ensuring safe behavior is critical for mod-ern autonomous cyber-physical systems. Control barrier functions (CBFs) are widely used to enforce safety in au-tonomous systems, yet their placement within networked control architectures remains largely unexplored. In this work, we investigate where to enforce safety in a net-worked control system in which a remote model predic-tive controller (MPC) communicates with the plant over a delayed network. We compare two safety strategies: i) a local myopic CBF filter applied at the plant and ii) predic-tive CBF constraints embedded in the remote MPC. For both architectures, we derive state-dependent disturbance tolerance bounds and show that safety placement induces a fundamental trade-off: local CBFs provide higher dis-turbance tolerance due to access to fresh state measure-ments, whereas MPC-CBF enables improved performance through anticipatory behavior, but yields stricter admissible disturbance levels. Motivated by this insight, we propose a combined architecture that integrates predictive and local safety mechanisms. The theoretical findings are illustrated in simulations on a planar three-degree-of-freedom robot performing a collision-avoidance task. 

Index Terms — Control Barrier Functions, Control over Communications, Control System Architecture, Networked Control Systems, Safety-Critical Control 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# SAFETY is paramount for cyber-physical systems such as autonomous vehicles operating in public spaces, or robots interacting with humans. Control barrier functions (CBFs) have emerged as a powerful framework for enforcing safety constraints by guaranteeing forward invariance of safe sets while minimally modifying nominal control inputs [1], [2], [3]. CBFs are commonly implemented as myopic, single-step safety filters acting at the plant. While effective in ensuring safety, purely local safety filters may lead to undesirable behavior such as late obstacle avoidance, loss of feasibility, or deadlock situations [4], [5]. To address these issues, recent work has integrated CBF constraints directly into model pre-dictive control (MPC) formulations [6], allowing safety to be considered within a predictive optimization framework. In many modern cyber-physical systems, including cloud robotics, autonomous vehicle fleets, and teleoperated robots, control is implemented in a networked architecture [7], where computationally intensive planning or optimization is per-formed remotely and low-level feedback control is executed

locally. While remote optimization enables complex predictive planning, it relies on delayed or predicted state information due to communication constraints [8]. This raises a fundamen-tal architectural question: where should safety mechanisms such as CBFs be placed in a networked control system? CBFs can either be enforced locally at the plant side, bene-fiting from fresh measurements but acting myopically, or em-bedded in a remote predictive controller, enabling anticipatory behavior but relying on predicted states affected by network delay and disturbances. While recent work has considered potential architectures for CBF in networked [9] or multi-rate [10] setups, the architectural implications of enforcing safety locally or remotely have not yet been systematically analyzed. In this paper, we investigate the placement of CBF-based safety filters in a networked control architecture consisting of a remote MPC and a locally executed controller. We analytically characterize the disturbance tolerance of two safety strategies: i) a local myopic CBF filter applied at the plant and ii) predictive CBF constraints embedded in the remote MPC. Our contributions in this work are threefold:  

> •

Firstly, we derive analytical disturbance tolerance bounds for the local and remote CBF placement.  

> •

Secondly, through formal comparison, we reveal an ar-chitectural trade-off between disturbance tolerance and predictive safety.  

> •

Thirdly, we propose a hybrid safety architecture combin-ing predictive and local safety mechanisms. Our theoretical findings are illustrated in simulations on a planar three-degrees-of-freedom robot performing a collision-avoidance task. The paper is organized as follows. Sec. II introduces the problem setup and preliminaries. In Sec. III, we present our results on the disturbance tolerance due to the specific placement of CBFs in a networked control system (NCS). In Sec. IV, we propose an approach to combine the predictive and myopic safety components before we present simulation results in Sec. V. Finally, we conclude the work in section VI.

Proposition 3 formalises the difference in disturbance tol-erance due to the safety placement. On the one hand, the remote architecture relies on predicted states, which accumu-late uncertainty due to delayed measurements and disturbance propagation. While the local CBF must only account for disturbances affecting the next state transition, yielding

On the other hand, even in the absence of delay, the multi-step enforcement of the barrier constraint along the MPC horizon introduces additional conservativeness through repeated con-traction (1 − γ)l, reducing the admissible disturbance level. The local CBF seems to be the advantageous architectural choice, since it profits from fresh state information and thus exhibits larger disturbance tolerance. By contrast, embedding the CBF into the remote MPC introduces conservativeness due to delay-dependent prediction mismatch and multi-step safety enforcement along the horizon. Yet, the price of larger local disturbance tolerance is myopic behavior, which may lead to late reactions, infeasibility, deadlock, or degraded task perfor-mance. The remote MPC-CBF instead improves performance and task completion through its predictive capabilities, but only under tighter admissible disturbance levels. This trade-off motivates the combined architecture introduced in the next section.

VI. C ONCLUSION 

This work demonstrates that the placement of CBFs in NCS fundamentally affects both safety and performance. We derived disturbance-tolerance bounds for local and remote CBF implementations and showed that predictive safety under delay yields stricter admissible disturbance levels. Simulations confirm that local CBFs provide superior robustness due to access to fresh state information, while remote MPC-CBF improves performance through anticipatory behavior, yet is more sensitive to disturbances. This establishes a fundamental trade-off between robustness and performance induced by architectural design. To address this limitation, we proposed a combined architecture that separates predictive performance from local safety enforcement. Our results indicate that this approach preserves safety under larger disturbances while re-taining favourable control performance and smoothness. From a design perspective, local CBFs are preferable in delay-and disturbance-dominated settings, whereas MPC-CBF is advantageous when disturbances are small, and performance is critical. The combined architecture provides a practical com-promise in uncertain networked environments. Future work includes analysing the implications of safety filter placement on the feasibility of the optimization problems, as well as validating the proposed architecture experimentally.
