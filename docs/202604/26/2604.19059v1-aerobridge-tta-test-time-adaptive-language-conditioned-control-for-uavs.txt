# AeroBridge-TTA: Test-Time Adaptive Language-Conditioned Control for UAVs 

Lingxue Lyu* University of Pennsylvania Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, United States 

lingxuelyu@alumni.upenn.edu 

Abstract — Language-guided unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) often fail not from bad reasoning or perception, but from execution mismatch : the gap between a planned trajectory and the controller’s ability to track it when the real dynamics differ from training (mass changes, drag shifts, actuator delay, wind). We propose AeroBridge-TTA, a language-conditioned control pipeline that targets this gap with test-time adaptation. It has three parts: a language encoder that maps the command into a subgoal, an adaptive policy conditioned on the subgoal and a learned latent, and a test-time adaptation (TTA) module that updates the latent online from observed transitions. On five language-conditioned UAV tasks under 13 mismatch conditions with the same domain randomization, AeroBridge-TTA ties a strong PPO-MLP baseline in-distribution and wins all 5 out-of-distribution (OOD) conditions, +22 .0 pts on average (62 .7% vs. 40 .7% ); the +8 .5 pt overall gain comes entirely from the OOD regime. A same-weights ablation that only changes the step size α shows the latent update itself is responsible for a 4.6× OOD lift. Code, checkpoints, and rollout videos: 

https://github.com/lyulingxue/AeroBridge-TTA .

Keywords— Unmanned aerial vehicles, language-conditioned control, test-time adaptation, domain randomization, reinforcement learning, sim-to-real. I. I NTRODUCTION 

Large language models (LLMs) make it tempting to com-mand robots in plain English instead of writing waypoints by hand [1], [2], [3]. For unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) the appeal is even stronger: an operator can just say “fly low to the target while avoiding the obstacle” [4], [5]. The reasoning and perception layers of these systems have improved a lot [6], [7], but one bottleneck is often ignored: execution mismatch . Even when the language mod-ule gives the right high-level plan, the low-level controller can fail when the real dynamics differ from training. Payload changes, damaged propellers, actuator delay, and wind all push the realised trajectory away from the intended one. The drone does not misunderstand the command; it just cannot fly the plan. Online adaptation has a long history in other robot do-mains, and the lesson is consistent. In humanoid robotics, recovering balance from a sudden push needs millisecond-scale strategy switching. One line of work uses hand contact to stabilise a falling humanoid via real-time optimal con-trol [8], and later realises the same strategy in closed loop on a physical robot [9]. Our contributions are: 1) Execution-mismatch hypothesis. We name execution mismatch as a primary failure mode of language-conditioned UAV control, separate from perception or reasoning errors, and we show that domain randomi-sation alone does not close it (Sec. III). 2) Structured language-to-control pipeline. A three-stage architecture, language encoder → subgoal →

adaptive policy, that separates semantic understanding from motor execution. We test AeroBridge-TTA on five language-conditioned tasks and 13 mismatch conditions, and compare it to a classical PID controller and to a strong PPO-MLP baseline trained with the same domain randomisation. II. R ELATED WORK 

Language-conditioned robot control. SayCan [3] grounds instructions in affordances; RT-2 [1] and PaLM-E [2] train vision-language-action models end-to-end; LM-Nav [17] reuses pre-trained models for outdoor navigation. Aerial work covers vision-and-language navigation [4] and LLM-based task planning [6], [5]. Most of these works assume the low-level controller is good enough, which is exactly what breaks under dynamics mismatch. 

UAV control and learning. Classical work uses geometric controllers [18] and trajectory optimisation [19]. RL has been applied to quadrotors [20] and to champion-level drone rac-ing [21], [22]. Neural-Fly [12] and Neural Lander [13] learn residual dynamics, but they are not language-conditioned. 

Sim-to-real and adaptation. Domain randomisation [23], [24] trains over a dynamics distribution. RMA [11] learns an adaptation module for legged robots. Test-time training [25] and self-supervised policy adaptation [26] update the model at deployment. Online calibration [16] corrects the dynamics for autonomous cars at test time. We bring this adaptation-at-execution idea to language-conditioned UAV control. 

Robust execution in robotics. The need for closed-loop adaptation is well known in humanoids, which must recover from disturbances in real time [8], [9], [10], and in balance problems [14]. Our take is the same: robustness under mismatch needs an adaptation step inside the controller, not only a smarter plan. III. P ROBLEM FORMULATION

DR and TTA are complementary. Inside the DR range, both methods are saturated and basically tied. The gap opens on OOD, where AeroBridge-TTA wins every condition (up to 7.7× on Combined-OOD). A latent that summarises the recent transition residuals is the right tool for perturbations DR cannot enumerate, especially when several axes (mass, drag, delay, wind) interact at once. Why the Combined-OOD gap is so large. Stacking mass, drag, delay, and wind together creates a regime that the baseline never sees, even with wide DR. AeroBridge-TTA does not need to have seen this exact combination at training time: as long as the residual signature is in the span of what 

fψ learned, the latent moves in a direction the policy already knows how to react to. This is why we observe a 7.7× gain on Combined-OOD, while Wind-strong (a single, mild axis) gives a much smaller gain. 

Limits. Adaptation cannot beat physical limits: at Mass +50% both methods fail because the rotors run out of thrust. A fixed α is fine inside the actuator envelope; gating α on residual magnitude is left for future work. The language frontend is drop-in (Eq. 5); a stronger LLM or VLA can replace ϕ without retraining the controller. The TTA head is about 6.5K parameters and one forward pass per step, suitable for 50 Hz real-time control, unlike gradient-based TTA which needs a backward pass each update [25]. Everything in this paper is in simulation; real flights would also need sensor noise, communication delay, and actuator nonlinearity. VII. C ONCLUSION 

AeroBridge-TTA targets execution mismatch in language-conditioned UAV control with test-time adaptation. A single language-conditioned model does 5 tasks at 97 .6% average SR. Under 13 mismatch conditions with the same DR, it ties PPO-MLP in-distribution ( ∆=0 ) and wins every OOD condition ( +22 .0 pts, 7.7× on Combined-OOD, +33 .3 on Mass +40% ). The same-weights ablation isolates the mecha-nism: raising α from 0 to 0.1 lifts average OOD success from 

16 .8% to 77 .5% . The execution-mismatch view and this TTA formulation should carry over to other language-conditioned robots that face dynamics they did not train on. REFERENCES [1] A. Brohan, N.
