# Planning Smooth and Safe Control Laws for a Unicycle Robot Among Obstacles 

Aref Amiri 1, Bas ¸ak Sakc ¸ak 1,2, and Steven M. LaValle 1

Abstract — This paper presents a framework for safe nav-igation of a unicycle point robot to a goal position in an environment populated with obstacles from almost any ad-missible state, considering input limits. We introduce a novel quadratic programming (QP) formulation to create a C∞-smooth vector field with reduced total bending and total turning. Then we design an analytic, non-linear feedback controller that inherently satisfies the conditions of Nagumo’s theorem, ensuring forward invariance of the safe set without requiring any online optimization. We have demonstrated that our controller, even under hard input limits, safely converges to the goal position. Simulations confirm the effectiveness of the proposed framework, resulting in a twice faster arrival time with over 50% lower angular control effort compared to the baseline. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Motion planning is concerned with finding a trajectory connecting an initial state with a goal state that avoids obstacles. However, this solution is open-loop. Therefore, applying (if available) the computed controls or following the computed trajectory may deviate from the goal. Feed-back motion planning avoids this limitation by generating a state-feedback control law over the free configuration space Cf ree [1], [2]. This control law can steer almost any admissible robot configuration to the goal, ensuring also some inherent robustness to disturbances. Classical methods for computing feedback laws to solve motion planning problems rely on artificial potential fields (APF) [3]. APF combine attractive and repulsive forces, moving the robot along the gradient of a potential function. However, they suffer from local minima (where attractive and repulsive forces cancel each other) and poor performance in narrow passages [4]. Navigation functions [5], a special class of APFs, overcome local minima; however, they are limited to simple environments. Considering determining a feedback law that is globally convergent to the goal, Lindemann and LaValle [2] proposed blending smooth vector fields computed over a convex de-composition of Cf ree . The main drawback of this approach is that the quality of the resulting integral curves is highly sensitive to the assignment of local vector fields and the underlying cell decomposition. This paper builds upon [2] and we propose a new formulation to assign cell vector fields with a convex QP. Because the vector fields are locally assigned, the choice of an appropriate cell decomposition is critical. Convex cell decomposition methods provide a structured way to partition the free configuration space, Cf ree , into a set of convex regions. Standard decomposition methods include trapezoidal cell decomposition [6] and simplicial decompositions (which our work is built upon) such as Delaunay triangulation (DT) [7]. In higher dimensions, where obstacle boundaries are rep-resented implicitly, or non-polygonal obstacles in 2D, volu-metric collision detection modules can be used to embed an approximate yet safe triangulation within Cf ree [8]. For an environment with polygonal obstacles, constrained Delaunay triangulation (CDT) is a particularly suitable approach as it can produce an exact cell decomposition, which can be done in O(n log n) [9]. CDT is a generalization that respects the obstacle boundaries by forcing the specified polygonal edges into the triangulation [10]. CDT, without the addition of extra vertices (Steiner points), can still produce thin triangles. Although using Steiner points can improve mesh quality, our framework can operate on any simplicial complex embedded in the free position space F.A successful navigation method should ensure that the robot reaches the goal while avoiding obstacles and respect-ing additional relevant constraints. control barrier functions (CBFs) are a powerful tool for providing formal safety guar-antees by ensuring that the state is forward invariant within a safe set [11]. Satisfying a CBF-based constraint implies safety, and this is typically imposed through a QP that works as a safety filter. The common approach in CBFs is to maintain a predetermined safe distance from obstacles [12], [13]. These approaches are practical for holonomic vehicles but are often conservative for non-holonomic systems, since the system is unable to avoid obstacles by controlling angular velocity, requiring more complex CBFs [14], [15]. In contrast to CBF methods that typically solve a QP at each step to find the control inputs, our analytic controller inherently provides a formal safety guarantee without requiring optimization. This paper presents a framework for constructing afeedback motion plan for a unicycle robot moving in a 2D workspace populated by obstacles. Our approach is to sequentially determine a guidance vector field over the

The core of our method is the well assignment of local vector fields across a simplicial decomposition embedded in the free space, F. This section details our optimization-based approach, which begins by reestablishing the foundational concepts upon which our formulation is based. 

A. Discrete Plan and Vector Field Constraints 

Let T = {∆1, ..., ∆N } be a simplicial complex embedded in F. To guide the robot motion globally, we first compute a high-level discrete plan over T . This is achieved by first constructing a connectivity graph where nodes correspond to the centroids of the 2-simplexes (triangles) and edges connect adjacent 2-simplexes. This construction results in a sparse graph with at most 3N/ 2 edges (undirected), where N is the finite number of simplexes embedded in F. A single-source shortest path algorithm, such as Dijkstra’s, can then be used to compute a successor mapping, s(i), for each simplex 

∆i ∈ T \ ∆G, in which ∆G is the 2-simplex containing the goal position. This mapping defines the unique successor 

2-simplex that the robot should transition to from ∆i to eventually reach the goal simplex ∆G.The foundation of our feedback plan is the assignment of a constant cell vector field, Vc,i , to each 2-simplex ∆i ∈ T .Each 2-simplex has three vertices, and each 1-dimensional face (edge), like the exit face (the face of a cell shared with its successor), has two vertices. Therefore, for a given exit face, there exists an opposite vertex ( vov,i ) that does not lie on the exit face. For a 2-simplex ∆i, the conical region (a wedge) is defined by two boundary vectors, {bi, 1, b i, 2},which point from vov,i to each of the two vertices of the exit face fexit ,i . See Figure 1 for an illustration. Choosing a cell vector field Vc,i pointing inside this conical region guarantees that the robot will go out from the exit face and satisfies the conditions of the definition of the cell vector field [2]. We recall this definition here. 

Definition 1. For a 2-simplex ∆i with an exit face fexit ,i , a cell vector field Vc,i is a smooth unit vector field on ∆i that satisfies three conditions: 

1) For each point p ∈ ∆i, there exists a q ∈ fexit ,i and 

α ∈ R such that Vc,i (p) = α(q − p).

2) Let h be a generalized voronoi diagram (GVD) [20] face with normal vector n. If Vc,i (p) · n = 0 for some 

p ∈ h, then Vc,i (p) · n = 0 for all p ∈ h.Fig. 1. Figure shows two adjacent 2-simplexes (triangles), ∆i and its successor ∆s(i). For cell ∆i, the candidate vector field Vc,i must lie within the conical region formed by the boundary vectors, bi, 1 and bi, 2. These vectors are defined by the opposite vertex vov,i and the vertices of the shared exit face fexit ,i .

3) The directed transition graph induced by this choice of vector fields is acyclic, and every path through this graph terminates at the node corresponding to the exit face. 

Proposition 1. A constant cell vector field Vc,i that is chosen to point within the conical region defined by the boundary vectors satisfies the conditions of Definition 1. Proof. As established in [21], any positive linear combina-tion of two boundary vectors {bi, 1, b i, 2} used to construct 

Vc,i will point toward the exit face fexit ,i . Since both Vc,i 

and n (the constant normal vector to a face of the GVD) are constant vectors, if Vc,i · n = 0 for some point on h, it is zero for all points on h. Because Vc,i points towards the exit face, any trajectory starting in ∆i will proceed monotonically toward fexit ,i and is guaranteed to intersect it. For the full proof, we refer the reader to [21].

The feedback term, even under saturation, reduces ϕ and the system correctly prioritizes reorientation. Now, if ϕ is a small value, the feedback law is consequently small, and saturation can happen when ˙θd is large. This happens when the robot is moving at high speed. In these situations, a mismatch between the commanded and required angular velocity will cause the robot to fall off the target manifold M, and |ϕ| will begin to increase. As |ϕ|

increases, the controller Eq. (12) reduces v. The reduction of v directly reduces the magnitude of ˙θd. This creates a self-regulating loop. When |ϕ| increases, v decreases, therefore, 

˙θd decreases and saturation most likely stops. Once saturation ends, the controller’s stability (from Proposition 4) takes over, ϕ is driven back to zero, and v increases again. pg . Since ω is always acting to reduce ϕ, the robot will not remain in a stopped state (unless p = pg ). The results in the next section confirm this claim. While our safety proof in Proposition 3 is intuitive, we show that our proposed analytic control laws (Eqs. (12) and (17)) inherently satisfy Nagumo’s theorem condition [19]. This demonstrates that our work achieves the same rigorous safety guarantee as CBF-QP methods, but avoids the computational overhead of solving an optimization problem at each control step. As we discussed in Proposition 3, non-exit face vector fields are assigned to point inward; therefore, on the boundary of the obstacles (considering polygonal obstacles and for non-polygonal obstacles, the non-exit face itself), points away. Therefore, when the robot is near the obstacles, having |ϕ| > π/ 2 with a forward motion can cause a collision. By the control law Eq. (12), robot stops the forward motion when |ϕ| ≥ π/ 2, hence we can define a safe set as all states when |ϕ| ≤ π/ 2. We prove that our safe set is not only forward invariant, but also globally attractive.

First, we discuss the proposed QP method for constructing vector fields. Our framework is based on the baseline method [2]. Lindemann and LaValle acknowledge that the quality of the resulting integral curves can be highly sensitive to cell decomposition and the assignment of the local vector fields. Their simple formulation, such as assigning cell vectors to point toward the exit face centroid and assigning inward normal vectors for non-exit faces, and outward normal vector for the exit face, produces integral curves with unnecessary bending. We should acknowledge that the baseline method works over any convex cell decomposition; therefore, post-processing the triangulation [23] to create larger convex cells can improve the results of their method, although this can be time-consuming. However, the code can be further optimized for more efficiency. Online execution is lightweight. For a given state, the method performs a point-location query, which requires O(n) time [18], followed by minimal calculation for the distances to the faces for the calculation of vector fields blending and controller evaluation. VII. C ONCLUSION 

We presented a framework for feedback motion planning and control for a non-holonomic unicycle robot. We intro-duced a novel QP formulation that globally optimizes the assignment of cell vector fields, generating a C∞-smooth vector field with measurably lower total bending and total turning. Building upon this vector field, we designed anonlinear controller, inspired by [18], to safely navigate the robot from almost any admissible state while respecting input limits. We proved that our controller inherently satis-fies safety conditions without requiring online optimization. Simulations confirmed the effectiveness of our method. Even under hard saturation limits, the robot safely converged to the goal. The synergy between the proposed vector field and controller resulted in significantly lower arrival times and reduced angular control effort compared to the baseline.
